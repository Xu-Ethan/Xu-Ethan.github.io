Question 1.

In the \(xy\)-plane, the curve with parametric equations \(x=\cos t\) and \(y=\sin t\), \(0\le t\le \pi\) has length

\begin{align}&(\text{A})3&&\boxed{(\text{B})\pi}&&(\text{C})3\pi&&(\text{D})\frac{3}{2}&&(\text{E})\frac{\pi}{2}\end{align}

Solution.

The arc length formula is given by the integral: \[L = \int_{a}^{b} \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2} dt\] In this case, \(a = 0\) and \(b = \pi\). Have the arc length: \begin{align*} L &= \int_{0}^{\pi} \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2} dt \\ &= \int_{0}^{\pi} \sqrt{\left(-\sin t\right)^2 + \left(\cos t\right)^2} dt \\ &= \int_{0}^{\pi} \sqrt{\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t} dt \\ &= \int_{0}^{\pi} 1 dt = \pi - 0= \pi \end{align*} Therefore, the length of the curve is \(\pi\).

Question 2.

Which of the following is an equation of the line tangent to the graph of \(y=x+e^x\) at \(x=0\)?

\begin{align}&(\text{A})y=x&&(\text{B})y=x+1&&(\text{C})y=x+2\\ &(\text{D})y=2x&&\boxed{(\text{E})y=2x+1}\end{align}

Solution.

Take the derivative to find the slope of the tangent line: \[\frac{dy}{dx} = 1 + e^x\] Evaluating this derivative at \(x = 0\) gives us: \[m=\left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{x=0} = 1 + e^0 = 1 + 1 = 2\] So, the slope of the tangent line is 2. Plugging in \(x = 0\) into the original equation, we get: \[y = 0 + e^0 = 0 + 1 = 1\] Thus, the point on the line \((x_1,y_1)=(0, 1)\). Recall the point-slope form of a linear equation: \[y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\] Plugging in the values, we have: \[y - 1 = 2(x - 0)\text{ i.e. }y = 2x + 1\]

Question 3.

If \(V\) and \(W\) are \(2\)-dimensional subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^4\), what are the possible dimensions of the subspace \(V\cap W\)?

\begin{align}&(\text{A}) \text{1 only}&&(\text{B}) \text{2 only}\\ &(\text{C}) \text{0 and 1 only}&&\boxed{(\text{D})\text{0, 1, and 2 only}}\\ &(\text{E})\text{0, 1, 2, 3, and 4}\end{align}

Solution.

The dimension formula for the sum of subspaces: $$\dim(V + M) = \dim V + \dim M - \dim(V \cap M)$$ Rearrange the formula we have: $$\dim(V \cap W) = \dim V + \dim W - \dim(V + W)$$ Substituting \(\dim V = 2\) and \(\dim W = 2\) gives: $$\dim(V \cap W) = 4 - \dim(V + W)$$ Since \(\dim(V + W)\in\{2,3,4\}\), compute respectively, follows \(\dim(V\cap W)\in\{0,1,2\}\).

Question 4.

Let \(k\) be the number of real solutions of the equation \(e^x+x-2=0\) in the interval \([0,1]\), and let \(n\) be the number of real solutions that are \(\underline{\text{not}}\) in \([0,1]\). Which of the following is true?

\begin{align}&(\text{A})k=0\text{ and }n=1&&\boxed{(\text{B})k=1\text{ and }n=0}\\ &(\text{C})k=n=1&&(\text{D})k>1\\ &(\text{E})n>1\end{align}

Solution.

Let \(f(x)=e^x+x-2\). Taking the derivative gives: $$f'(x) = e^x + 1$$ Since \(f'(x) = e^x + 1\) is always positive that \(f(x)\) can have at most one real root in the interval \((0,1)\). Since \(f(0) = -1 < 0 < e - 1 = f(1)\), by the Intermediate Value Theorem, there is at least one real root \(c\in (0,1)\) that \(f(c)=0\). That there is exactly one real solution in the interval \([0,1]\) gives \(k = 1\).

Consider two cases for \(x\not\in[0,1]\) as the following: $$\text{For \(x < 0\) have \(f(x) < f(0) < 0\).}$$ $$\text{For \(x > 1\) have \(f(x) > f(1) > 0\).}$$ This is because \(f(x)\) is strictly increasing. That there is no real solution outside of the interval \([0,1]\). Therefore, we can conclude that \(n=0\).

Question 12.

Let \(A\) be a \(2\times 2\) matrix for which there is a constant \(k\) such that the sum of the entries in each row and each column is \(k\). Which of the following must be an eigenvector of \(A\)?

\begin{align} &\text{I.}\begin{pmatrix} 1\\ 0 \end{pmatrix} &&\text{II.}\begin{pmatrix} 0\\ 1 \end{pmatrix} &&\text{III.}\begin{pmatrix} 1\\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{align} \begin{align}&(\text{A})\text{I only}&&(\text{B})\text{II only}\\ &\boxed{(\text{C})\text{III only}}&&(\text{D})\text{I and II only}\\ &(\text{E})\text{I, II, and III}\end{align}

Solution.

Given that \(A\) is a \(2\times2\) matrix. Let: $$ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} $$ for some \(a,b,c,d\), with the condition we have \begin{align} &1.~a + b = k&&2.~c + d = k\\ &3.~a + c = k&&4.~b + d = k \end{align} Equation 1 and 4 give \(a = b\) and 1 and 3 give \(b=c\) that we can rewrite \(A\) as: $$ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix} $$ An eigenvector of a matrix \(A\) is a vector that, when multiplied by \(A\), only changes in scale. In other words, if \(v\) is an eigenvector of \(A\), there exists a scalar \(\lambda\) such that \(Av = \lambda v\). Let's check each given vector:

  • For \(\text{I. }\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\), we have: $$ A\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \end{pmatrix} $$
  • Similarly, for \(\text{II. }\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\), we get: $$ A\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} b \\ a \end{pmatrix} $$
  • Lastly, for \(\text{III. }\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\), we get: $$ A\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = (a+b)\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$
Hence only III. is an eigenvector where \(\lambda=a+b\).

Question 13.

A total of \(x\) feet of fencing is to form three sides of a level rectangular yard. What is the maximum possible area of the yard, in terms of \(x\)?

\begin{align}&(\text{A})\frac{x^2}{9}&&\boxed{(\text{B})\frac{x^2}{8}}&&(\text{C})\frac{x^2}{4}\\ &(\text{D})x^2&&(\text{E})2x^2\end{align}

Solution.

Let \(a\) be the length of the fence parallel to the house and \(b\) be the length of the fence on the other two sides giving \( a + 2b = x\) so \( a = x - 2b.\) Therefore the area is \(A = b(x - 2b) = bx - 2b^2\), that \(\frac{d}{db}bx-2b^2=x-4b\). The area has maximum when \(x-4b=0\) i.e. \(b=\frac{x}{4}\) which follows that \(a=x-2\frac{x}{4}=\frac{x}{2}\). The maximum possible area of the yard in terms of \(x\) is \(a\cdot b = \frac{x}{4}\frac{x}{2}=\frac{x^2}{8}\).

Question 16.

What is the volume of the solid formed by revolving about the \(x\)-axis the region in the first quadrant of the \(xy\)-plane bounded by the coordinate axes and the graph of the equation \(y=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x^2}}\)?

\begin{align}&(\text{A})\frac{\pi}{2}&&(\text{B})\pi&&(\text{C})\frac{\pi^2}{4}\\ &\boxed{(\text{D})\frac{\pi^2}{2}}&&(\text{E})\infty\end{align}

Solution.

Find the volume of Solid of Revolution as following: \begin{align} &\pi\int_0^\infty f(x)^2dx\\ =&\pi\int_0^\infty\frac{1}{1+x^2}dx\\ =&\pi \left( \lim_{{x \to \infty}} \arctan(x) - \arctan(0) \right)\\ =& \pi(\frac{\pi}{2}-0)=\frac{\pi^2}{2} \end{align} Hence the volume of the solid is \(\pi^2/2\).

Question 18.

Let \(V\) be the real vector space of all real \(2\times 3\) matrices, and let \(W\) be the real vector space of all real \(4\times 1\) column vectors. If \(T\) is a linear transformation from \(V\) \(\underline{\text{onto}}\) \(W\), what is the dimension of the subspace \(\{v\in V: T(v)=0\}\)?

\begin{align}&\boxed{(\text{A})2}&&(\text{B})3&&(\text{C})4&&(\text{D})5&&(\text{E})6\end{align}

Solution.

Denote the subspace \(\{v \in V : T(v) = 0\}\) as \(\ker(T)\), which is also known as the null space or kernel of the linear transformation \(T\). The dimension of \(\ker(T)\) can be determined using the rank-nullity theorem: $$\dim(\text{Im} T)+\dim(\ker T)=\dim(\text{Domain}(T))$$ Have \(\dim(\ker T)=2\cdot 3-4\cdot 1=2\).

Question 19.
